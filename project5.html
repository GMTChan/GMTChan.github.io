MEA aims to be a network that connects autonomous vehicle (AV) companies to drivers' behavioral data. Users download an app that provides incentives in exchange for data which contributes to AV development. The app will track acceleration, braking, turns, lane changes, average speed relative to traffic, driving duration/time and phone usage, all of which will be sent to a cloud database when the smartphone connects to wifi. The data is open source to any institution and anonymized to ensure privacy. Users will have the option to cash out earnings into their bank account or donate to charities. This creates a positive feedback loop where drivers can earn money for driving and AV companies can improve their algorithm for increased road safety.

The Process

During my last year at UCSD, I enrolled in a Civic Design course, which collaborated with Design Forward San Diego to produce a city wide competition that centered around finding innovative solutions to improve the experience of moving around in San Diego. Participants were encouraged to use the human centered design process on 4 main topics: safety on the street, accessibility, commuter experience, and autonomous vehicles. I teamed up 4 other individuals to compete for the grand prize of $5,000 and an opportunity to network with industry professionals.

Week 1: Research

Our initial topic of interest was in improving the safety on the roads; this was guided by San Diego’s Vision Zero initiative which aims to reduce the number of commuter casualties to 0. The first step was to understand San Diego and its users’ needs. We began by observing drivers at the “Fatal 15” intersections, which are famous for the highest number of pedestrian injuries and deaths in the past 15 years.

We saw many close calls where drivers didn’t notice pedestrians while performing right turns. There was a general pattern where drivers often rushed into the crosswalk while making these turns, then many would stop abruptly upon the realization of any pedestrians, but this reaction was usually delayed. They often looked left for cars but not right, possibly because there were no incoming cars from the right. We also noticed great deal of pedestrians J walking to make it onto the bus.

Close call

While at these intersections, we conducted several interviews, asking J walkers about their general commuting patterns. There was a broad agreement in that the distance between the crosswalks were too far. They also wished that crosswalks did better in catching a driver’s attention. Some suggested a lit up crosswalk and others wanted to make the cross walk signal more salient.

An enthusiastic interviewee bought up a driver tracking service by Progressive, where one’s driving patterns affected their insurance rates. She claimed that if more drivers adopted this, they would drive more carefully. Her friend was against this concept because she thought that driving was “too reactionary and situational.” She said that speeding, braking hard and quick lane switches doesn’t really determine the quality of a driver.

Field Notes

We also conducted an online survey asking people of their general driving patterns. The replies indicated that people knew distractions like cell phones and conversations were the main causes for accidents, but they continue to text and drive.

Survey Results

Driver Confidence

A large portion of the users thought they were good drivers, which was peculiar because more than half of them were involved in an accident. Our insight was that this confidence in driving causes people to put their own priorities over the safety of others because the other party is usually at fault. Being in a car lowers empathy because drivers don’t see each other as people, but as vehicles. So how do we make people more mindful of other drivers?

Week 2: Ideation

Thus, we began to ideate on ways to increase the feeling of empathy between drivers on the road. Based on the research, the main user needs were privacy, better communication, and incentives to slowly change people to adapt more cognizant driving behaviors.

We struggled a lot during this phase because empathy is such an intrinsic concept that varies from person to person. This made it difficult to ideate for solutions that would affect a broad user base. We needed to take a different angle towards improving safety on the road.

Needfinding

Fortunately, our professor gave us some insights on his current research topic. He found that driving become more passive over time because drivers get accustomed to technology and the environment. They slowly develop motor functions that react almost subconsciously to environmental cues. This can be linked to the survey results where a large number of users had high confidence in their driving abilities. Familiarity = confidence. Thus, we had to figure out how to break drivers out of this passive state of driving, possibly by incorporating ways to alert them of their surroundings.

Double Diamond

As we diverged deeper into the problem of passive/distracted driving, we noticed that many of the causes were due the driver’s dependency on vision. We converged on this point and began ideating for solutions that incorporated other senses to reduce the cognitive load off of sight. This stage was tough because many of our ideas were already integrated into semi-AV cars. There was also the concern of overstimulation or even increased distractions.

We came across a DMV report on all AV related accidents; more than 90% of which were caused by humans. The AVs make the correct decisions, but in such an irregular fashion that human drivers are unable to react in time.

As we progress towards a society driven by AV’s, there will be a long transitional period where both AVs and human-driven cars are on the road. Human errors on the road could be dealt with safely if AVs were supplied with sufficient data about how humans react to unexpected situations. Is there a way to get drivers to extend their driving data to AV companies?

Prototype ideation

Problem Statement: A challenge that AVs are facing is how to coexist with human drivers on the road. Companies need an abundance of data on regular human driving patterns to create a safe transitionary period in which AVs and humans share the road.

Our current idea revolved around a service that connects AV companies to users’ driving behaviors. AV companies will pay drivers in exchange for this data depending on how much time driven.

Every week, we had a guest lecture from an industry professional who would also spend time giving us feedback. We learned that companies like Uber and Tesla open source their drivers’ data, so it may not be feasible to assume that companies would pay for it.

We decided to make the data open sourced and pitched the solution as a nonprofit organization, where driver incentives are paid by donations. Two of my teammates worked in non-profit organizations and had business experience with the grant process, market growth, and funding.

Mr. Robinson liked quality of the data being tracked because the current data available to AV companies is skewed, coming from “super-users” such as Uber drivers and Tesla owners, who do not represent the average driver. Longitudinal data from everyday users over time can reveal interesting fluctuations in driving patterns and trends.

Feedback from Scott Robinson
(FreshForm CEO & Board President of Design Forward)

Primary Stakeholders: AV companies and academic institutions in need of driving data for AV research. They get access to an abundance of organic data from a variety of locations, letting them develop more advanced AVs for safer roads.

Secondary Stakeholders: Everyday drivers. In the short term, they are able to generate extra revenue and in the long run, daily commutes will not only improve safety for them but also for future generations

Each member was responsible for interviewing 10 potential stakeholders. Every single college student said they would use the app. The preferred minimum amount of incentive averaged at 58¢ per hour of driving data.

Of all the professionals we interviewed (mainly professors and school employees), only a handful said they were willing to use the service. When asked why not they said they didn’t care about AVs and the incentive isn’t a large enough sum for them to risk their privacy. Regardless, this is still significant because research shows that drivers between ages 20 and 24 cause the most accidents on the road (McClellan Law).

Week 3: Prototyping

Our competitors were companies like Progressive, Dash, Automatic, and Zubie, who have devices that pull information from the OBD-II port of cars. Their participants must purchase an additional, physical plug-in device.

MEA is unique because our solution doesn’t require monetary commitment and primarily uses smartphone capabilities for data collection.

The novelty in MEA lies in targeting longitudinal data collected from everyday drivers, geared towards improving the development of AV artificial intelligence. This data enables a visualization of individual driving trends over time, giving the dataset high ecological validity.

I had the opportunity to pitch this idea to a software engineer from Uber at a job fair. He saw the value behind the data we were trying to collect, but felt that it would only work if we got massive amounts. Thus, a potential pitfall of MEA is that the effectiveness of the data depends on how many people adopt it.

One idea was to track this data by using cameras built into stop lights. They would track a driver’s license plate if they opt in and users can log onto a website to see everything. Clearly, there are huge cost and feasibility issues with this idea.

Our other option was to use sensors from mobile devices. GPS technology can track speed, acceleration, and braking patterns based on location services. As for micro-behaviors like lane changes and merges, the phone’s accelerometer and gyroscope are able to detect sensitive rates of change on a 3-dimensional plane by measuring linear acceleration of movement and angular rotational velocity.

What data improves AI in AV

Data Visualization

Several of our professors suggested us to incorporate additional data visualization features for the drivers. This adds a sense of ownership to the data they collect and may even encourage them to drive safer. Users can also see which institutions are using their data and what projects they have contributed to.

We focused on making a simple interface that centered around generating money; the statistical visualization was secondary. We drew inspiration from Venmo and Sweatcoin.

We didn’t encounter any problems in our usability tests and users figured out how to navigate the interface pretty quickly. This may be because the interface follows the familiar design shown in most bank apps.

A guest lecturer suggested us to incorporate an option to donate to a charity to attract users that didn’t care about the money incentive. This adds an intrinsic dimension to the service that gives people the opportunity to feel good about themselves for simply driving.
