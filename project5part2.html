Week 5: Reflection

After the D4SD, we began analyzing our critiques from the judges. The main problems were feasibility, marketing cost, funding, user demographic representation, consumer motivation, enterprise motivation and accuracy & consistency of data. Our team highlighted a problem that would occur with the introduction of AVs; however, we were unable to clearly provide a solution that encompasses our problem statement: “How can we better facilitate communication between human drivers and AVs.”

Feedback from D4SD Judges

We decided to go back to the need finding phase and research more on the current breakdowns. Our hearts were still attached to MEA and we didn’t want to completely abandon the topic of AV accidents yet. We found that California’s DMV had recorded all AV related accidents within the past 3 years. After reading through all the reports, we found an interesting fact: majority of the accidents were either rear end or left-turn related.

After looking through numerous articles and studies we realized that rear ends belonged to a much bigger and more dangerous story: distracted driving. Every year, over 391,000 people are injured due to distracted driving. In 2015 alone, there were 3,477 accidents from distracted driving. We discovered that in 58% of teen crashes, distraction was a key factor. Rather than just focusing on teen crashes, we kept looking into distracted driving across the entire population and what we found was interesting: both novel and experienced drivers perform the same behavior while driving (such as texting, talking on the phone, and eating). But in a study done by Geoffrey Underwood (2014), after conducting an eye tracking experiment on both novel and experienced drivers, he discovered that new drivers have not developed the necessary mental models to deal with such habits while driving. As a result, teen crash rates are a lot higher than any other group.

Statistics

We were able to assign the causes of most accidents into 3 main reasons: Distracted driving, bad habits, cognitive overload. With this in mind, we decided on a new problem statement:

“How can we encourage active driving depending on the specific drivers’ habits?”

Week 7: Prototyping & User Testing

We were inclined towards testing solutions that relied on either visual, auditory, or tactile cues because it seemed more practical than taste and smell. One such solution was The Onion Light.

Cars on the street decelerate at different rates depending on the different pressures applied on the brake pedal; however, conventional tail lights only communicate that the car is slowing down. We believed that communicating the deceleration rate may be helpful since so many accidents are rear-end related.

We imagined cars with sensors on the back to capture the acceleration and deceleration of the car directly behind. The intensity of braking relative to both cars’ speeds then will be analyzed and the appropriate break light intensity will be displayed to the driver behind. The prototype was a graduated brake light with different colors (ranging from yellow to red) to communicate different appropriate braking intensities.

Onion Light Prototype

To gauge the usability of the solution, we first needed to find out if people would understand the meaning behind the new light. We showed the animation to drivers and asked “what do you think the lights communicate?” and “what purpose does it serve?” To our surprise, most of them responded that they were not sure if it had to do with braking, signaling, or something else. Some also related it to emergency lights which is very far from what we were trying to communicate. Around the same time, we also found out that modifying brake lights and graduated brake lights are actually illegal in most of North America.

The other solution we tested was The Vibrating Seat. We saw that drivers had to interact with and stay aware of so many different dynamic elements on the road but they can only look forward. Utilizing tactile cues, we imaged cars installed with sensors around it, communicating information on the surroundings by vibrating different parts of the driver’s seat. For example, the buttocks area would prompt you to slow down if you are not aware that you are tailgating someone. Different parts of the back will also represent different objects relative to the car.

Vibrating Seat Prototype

We tested the prototype with 15 users, aiming to figure out if people could actually distinguish between the different vibrations quickly enough, remember what they mean, and carry out the appropriate response. Across the board, users agreed that it was too difficult to distinguish between the vibrations on different parts of the back while driving; they could distinguish the difference pretty accurately while parked though.

This reminded us of the theory of 2 point discrimination from another cognitive science course. This means that there are parts of the human body that are less sensitive than others and as a result make it more difficult to distinguish tactile touches and vibrations. Many users also said that they found the vibrations distracting

Week 8: MIA & Competitive Analysis

After honing in on visual and tactile senses, we decided to see if we could use audio as a way to help drivers. Inspired by Iron Man’s Jarvis, a computing system similar to Google Home, we came up with an auditory prototype that not only alerts drivers to potential obstacles, but also acts as a teaching assistant to new drivers to enforce good driving habits.

So why was this idea better than a visual assistant? Since driving already inflates cognitive load, we hypothesized that providing another visual focus for the driver would only be more distracting. And in our research, we were right: visual feedback while driving actually increases driver reaction times by 94.4%.

Furthermore, we discovered that there were two kinds of listening: passive and active. The main example of active is when you are engaging in a conversation with someone. Active listening causes a decrease in activation in the parietal lobe — the brain region responsible for motor control. Research has shown that engaging in active listening causes driver’s’ reaction times to increase by 13.84%.

Passive listening includes listening to music or your GPS and only increases your reaction time by 1.94%. This meant that having an audio assistant in your car could provide more contextual information about the road while minimizing distractions.

Audio Statistics

With our research and feedback from the judges, we decided to take MEA to a new direction: MIA (My Instructional Assistant). MIA helps build good driving habits with an AI teaching assistant that reduces visual cognitive load by providing real time audio feedback on the surroundings.

Instead of creating safer roads by promoting data mining for AV companies, we decided to focus on the human aspect. Rather than just using phones to track drivers, we will utilize car sensor technology: Ultrasonic, radar, lidar, and eye-tracking technology which would sense not only the acceleration and braking of the car, but also blind spots, obstacles, proximity to other cars, and driver’s gaze. This will generate a more accurate dataset that's needed to train an AI to teach drivers and quickly analyze dynamic situations.

But what about our competitors? If our inspiration came from Iron Man, surely that meant there were others out there with similar concepts.

First, we went head to head with Microsoft’s Cortana: Cortana is a digital assistant that was to be implemented into BMW and Nissan. The assistant is voice controlled, but could provide suggestions to the driver without having to prompt it, such as detecting traffic and suggesting an alternative route. Cortana is being marketed mainly as a virtual assistant that increases productivity by connecting drivers to their apps via an auditory system. It doesn't try to help the driver improve their behaviors.

Week 9: Features & Usability Testing

For the prototype, we used the Voice Memo app on the iPhone to record two types of phrases: Real Time Alerts and Safety Reminders. The entire feature set of MIA can be found here.

We ran usability tests by sitting in the back of the users' car and triggering such phrases when the activation conditions were met. The testing script can be found here.

Example Phrases

Usability Tests

The main feedback we got was that users wanted the ability to customize the frequency of the phrases. Some users found certain phrases more usefull than others. Users also thought it'd be useful to have an accompanying app which visualizes their driving trends.

Feedback

To increase customizability, we designed an AI system that adapts to different driving behaviors. Users wanted the option to turn on/off specific phrases. We agreed that they should have the ability to do this for the real time alerts; however, letting them enable/disable safety reminders would result in letting users abuse certain violations, which wouldn't improve bad driving habits.

We decided to let users modify the frequency of safety reminders, but the AI will automatically scale the frequency of the phrases back up depending on the severity and frequency of the violation. The AI will also scale down the frequency of phrases if the driver shows improvement.

Feedback on Customizability

The users also saw potential for improvement in the syntax of some of the phrases. They preferred shortened phrases with more contextual information and instructions on what action they should be performing.
