Context

Nanome is a startup funded under UCSD's incubator program, the Basement. The product is a collaborative virtual reality platform for designing atomic, molecular, mathematical, and nanoscale visualizations. I worked under the Matryx team, which helped VR users collaborate through the MTX blockchain on the Ethereum network. More information can be found here. Matryx is currently in alpha 2 and can be found here.

I made over 150 wireframes with Adobe XD. I won’t include all iterations/designs for both of our sanities.

Research

Full competitive analysis can be found here. The highlights:

  • Competitors had a public interface to show everyone’s profile activity
  • Usually one winner per challenge
  • Smart contracts were peer reviewed
  • Challenges were more focused on programing a product instead of R&D
  • Github integration
  • Easy account creation/login process through Metamask

We came up with 10 possible personas. For the MVP we focused on early adopters: people who firmly believe in and understand blockchain technology regardless of the volatile state of the crypto industry.

A few personas

Primary stakeholders: Pharma Institutions who want to solve real world problems but don’t have a platform to obtain shared knowledge or hidden talent

Secondary stakeholders: Scientists who need help with research but don’t have the resources or a team because of location and funding limitations.

Being in a fast paced startup environment, the CEO insisted that I don’t follow standard UX methodology (user testing then lofi sketches/wires with iterative usability testing towards hifi). I was told to create as many hifi iterations as possible and the team would decide which ones to move forward with.

Usability Tests

To make this huge case study more coherent, I wrote it in a different order than how the internship progressed. I performed one round of usability testing with 9 users in the middle of the internship and will discuss the findings in each section below.

The survey I used to recruit the users is here. The goals was to see how familiar the user was with blockchain technology and computer interfaces.

  • Novice: not familiar with blockchain technology
  • Intermediate: CS background and some familiarity
  • Expert: Fluent with both current state of cryptocurrency and how the blockchain data structure works.

The usability testing plan is here. I recruited 4 novice, 3 intermediate, and 2 expert users. I used the functional Alpha 1 MVP (04/12/18) to test already implemented functions (create account, enter tournament, & submit). I used a prototype to test allocating rewards to winners from the host’s point of view but I can’t link it through this case study because I don’t have the pro version of Adobe XD. I have a local version that can be shown during the interview.

Main findings:

  • Confusion with the modal design when going through long use cases (creating a tournament)
  • Confusion with unlocking a tournament because there wasn’t a status indicator
  • Confusion between round reward and total reward
  • Wanted a FAQ or hints
  • Confusion with the icons- words could be better
  • Confusion in creating a tournament- specifically the subject of “rounds” per tournament

Live Tournaments

My initial designs followed a list-like design pattern that many competitors had. It’s meant to include as much information as possible so that advanced users could navigate through the interface more quickly.

Live Tournaments v1

Due to limited resources, I could only user test on UX classmates and friends interested in cryptocurrency. Feedback:

  • Wanted more space between the listings
  • Wanted visual indicators like pictures
  • Wanted category distinction
  • Wanted short description of the tournament

After running the usability tests, users reported that the interface seemed too cluttered. They also said that there should be more emphasis on the tournament description so that they could quickly determine whether or not they could participate. After a meeting, we decided to move forward with the last iteration because it better aligned with the feedback.

Tournament Details

Important factors:

  • Tournament summary
  • Prior submissions
  • Previous winners
  • Rounds number, reward, remaining time
  • Clarification on how to enter a tournament (and it’s costs)

Each tournament is broken into multiple rounds and each round can have multiple winners. After the winners are picked, that round’s submissions are accessible by anyone; this is how the community works together towards a solution. The blockchain eliminates the possibility of plagiarism because all submissions are cross referenced with the data on the network.

Tournament Details v1

My initial designs were modal based but I soon learned this wouldn’t work because:

  • Programming limitation: stored data on the fields (Creating a Tournament task) disappear after opening another use case.
  • Programming limitation: slow load time when all data is stored in modals of one page.
  • Usability issue: modal flows for completing processes are confusing.

Second Iteration

We also figured that each round would have a different description so I added the feature to see prior round tournament details.

Create Tournament

The most difficult part was designing an interaction for tournament creation. A technical requirement was that the user had to first pay to become a peer on the network to add data onto it.

  • How should the user allocate MTX? Per each round? Should it be percentage based?
  • Should every round be an equal percentage?
  • When do they allocate this value?
  • What if they want to change the reward due to inadequate submissions?

Create Tournament v1

v3

The user struggled harder than expected when trying to understand how rounds worked so we broke down the process with a stepped progress interaction. The team decided to push towards a full screened interface rather than a modal because:

  • Modal design was prone to user misclicking and exiting the tournament creation process.
  • Using a full screen design also let the user see which step they were at in the process which created a smoother user experience.

v5

We incorporated a status indicator/notification system to make it more clear that you can't unlock or become a peer right away. The blockchain needs to process the transaction, which can take up to several minutes depending on how populated the network is.

Rewards

To reduce calculations when allocating rewards per round, the initial designs were percentage based. At first, the tournament host has the ability to reward 100% of the round reward to anyone. As the host continues to allocate the reward, the 100% is relative to the remaining matryx tokens left in the reward pot.

Allocating Rewards v1

After some discussion, we changed the round reward system (where the user decides how much reward must be allocated in each round when they create the tournament) to a minimum reward per round in the later iterations.

  • This was because of the edge case where there were no viable solutions submitted for that round. We don’t want to force the host to give rewards to unsatisfying solutions. Having a minimum reward per round makes the creation process easier and tournament results more flexible.
  • For example, if someone completely solves the solution in the first round, then all of the reward can go to them.

Intellectual Property

Since data in immutable on the blockchain, it’s easy to determine the owner of any piece of IP. The CEO had a vision to make a marketplace where people can trade the ownership of the “solutions” or IP with MTX tokens. This will enable big pharmaceutical & research companies to obtain licenses through a decentralized network and give commonday researchers or students an opportunity to make money.

During my last few days in the internship, we discussed about integrating a reputation system in the product. This is so that the community could self correct itself through reporting others if some managed to obtain intellectual property that wasn’t theirs. Learnings

I organized this case study by the product's features so that it would make sense; however, the actual timeline was very messy. I entered very early into the project and the team didn’t have a concrete vision of the direction of the product. For example, there was no onboarding, intellectual property marketplace, or reputation system in the beginning; these and many more features were decided on as I was designing the interface.

This experience was stressful but also extremely rewarding because I had a lot of control over the product’s direction. I love being involved in the content discussions and I learned so much about how to make my designs fit existing APIs because of the limited development resources.

I also gained a lot of experience with hifi prototyping because I had to produce new wireframes for development within hours after discussion. I do wish that I was able to incorporate a more user centered design process. I hope that I created a good foundation for Nanome’s future designers to iterate on.
