Context

Our PMs and designers felt that the global conclusion page needed improvement. My role was to research and redesign a “lighter” conclusion interface which works with Workday’s 50,000+ business tasks.

Original Design

Initial concerns:

  • There are a lot of empty and duplicate fields when expanded.
  • Users mentioned of low confidence in task completion.
  • Information isn't arranged in a visually appealing way.

Our Ireland team shipped a quick-fix design for the recruitment use case right before I started. Recruiters were an edge case because they went through dozens applications a day. They wanted something lighter because the full paged conclusion required them to click Done every time, which they felt was tedious.

Recruitment's Conclusion Design

My job was to work backwards from this specific use case and broaden their design to fit the requirements of multiple use cases.

Goals:

  • Research the problem, does this need to be fixed?
  • Ensure that users feel confident that they’ve successfully completed a task.
  • Maintain accessibility guidelines.
Quantitative Data

Since the conclusion page was scaled across all of Workday's tasks, I needed quantitative data on how different users interacted with it. Some questions I had:

  • How long did users stay on the conclusion page?
  • Did users use the related links on the right hand side?
  • What do users usually click on this page?
  • Where do users navigate to after finishing a BP?
  • How many tasks do users typically complete in one sitting?
  • Do they expand Details & Process? Is this task specific?

Unfortunately, data science confirmed that analytics was not set up in the conclusion page because of limitations in Workday's tech stack.

I surveyed designers and PMs, asking how their users interacted with the conclusion page. It consisted of open ended questions like: How do you see the current conclusion page being used?

Survey Data

Recurring Comments:

  • Users had low confidence in task completion- is the task completed on this page or after clicking `Done`?
  • Users reported being confused as to where they’re taken after clicking 'Done.'
  • The conclusion pages look too similar for multi stepped business processes so some users forget which step they’re in.
Going Broad

I interviewed designers from different teams and walked through a handful of user flows using preset personas to understand how different users might feel.

Mapping User Flows

Problem Statement: "High volume users feel burdened by a full page conclusion and need a simpler confirmation notice to save time".

My PM and I aligned on these principles for the initial designs:

  • Bring the content closer together and emphasize the status in header
  • Visually differentiate the conclusion page from the typical Workday page layout
  • Show context on the next page in the flow when the user clicks 'Done'
  • Test multiple interactions (toast notification, modal)

Lofi Wireframes

I brought the designs into office hours to get feedback from the creative directors:

  • They liked the modal version the most because it gave context to the page that would appear after clicking “Done”
  • Added a checkmark visual for the toast to increase visibility for accessibility users
  • Moved the toast to the top center because pages had action buttons on the right
Usability Testing

Data science provided the most used tasks on the platform. I categorized them based on how much manual input was required, the time it took to complete, and the business impact of each task. The top tasks for each category would be used for usability testing.

Versions to test

I recruited 15 users, 5 for each difficulty, and created a testing script (found here). Each user ran through their task 3 times and were either shown the original, slim, or modal conclusion page at the end.

Goals:

  • Test the validity of the observations pointed out by Workday employees in the earlier survey.
  • What actions do users typically take when shown a conclusion page?
  • What is/isn't important important on the conclusion page?
  • Are there any common behaviors across the categories?

Original Design

Findings for current full page design:

  • First time users thought this was an error page because of all the white space
  • Users look at the center (Details and Process) first, not the header on the left hand side
  • Almost every user prefered the Details and Process tab to be expanded. Default: not enough information, but expanding Details & Process: too much information
  • Empty fields, case #, overall process contained information that users didn’t know what to do with or cared about
  • Users wanted more visual indicators to show task completion

Modal Design

Findings for modal design:

  • Faster response time- users may have absorbed the details faster because the content was condensed.
  • Navigation confusion- users expected the modal to pop up on current page instead of redirecting to a new page and appearing.
  • Users wanted more visual indicators for success (like a green check mark)
  • The person directly up next is almost always the same person - redundant. Users doing medium/hard tasks cared about who was further down the approval chain
  • Easy/medium users didn't click View Details. Users doing hard task did
  • Users wanted instructions on how to revisit the confirmation after clicking Done

Slim Design

Findings for slim design:

  • Easy & medium tasks- users said that they only clicked on it to expand because it was their first time- they probably wouldn’t if they got used to the interface.
  • Difficult tasks- users wanted confirmation details
  • Users felt that the notification should stay there until an action is taken (rather than time based)

General Findings:

  • Slim and modal version performed better than the full conclusion for easy and medium difficulty tasks
  • Full page conclusion performed better for difficult tasks
  • The number of people who preferred the slim vs. modal was about equal
  • Data from Dublin: HR professionals tend to look at the 'details and process' section of conclusion pages, as it's important for them to see who's up next in the approval chain. Not only who's directly up next, but who's after that person and maybe even a third approver.

New Problem Statement: High volume users and users completing easy/medium difficulty tasks feel burdened by a full page conclusion because there is too much information. They need a condensed confirmation notice that not only makes them feel confident but also saves them time.

Pivot

Seeing that there was no preference over the slim versus the modal design, I took the feedback and created a hybrid design:

  • Integrated a business process chain to give more context on the following steps
  • Increased salience of completion indicators
  • Put some context on where the confirmation could be found (always sent to the archived mailbox)
  • An advantage of this design over the pure modal was that it saves a click
  • Designed the notification expand on hover to speed up the perception of the interaction
  • Removed Do Another and Related Links because prior users didn’t use it.
  • Technically, users can still do another or complete related tasks because the notification pops up on the page before starting the business process

More Testing

I tested on 10 different users and the testing script is linked here. I tested both the hybrid design and the full page conclusion design. My goal was to measure the validity of the new design and provide further research for the designer who would take up this project after my internship was over.

Findings for current full page design:

  • Similar results to the first round of testing- users spent more time on this page
  • Liked how D&P was already expanded- didn't like the blank fields
  • Users wanted the action buttons to be closer together.

Findings for hybrid design:

  • Faster task completion time
  • Expense Report users didn't care about approval chain, Change Job users did

General Findings:

  • 8 users prefered the hybrid design and 2 prefered the original full design
  • Status of submission and a brief summary of submission remains as the top two factors of a conclusion page
Learnings

Unfortunately, my internship came to an end before I could test this design on more use cases. It was difficult to scale a design to work with thousands of other tasks with snippets of qualitative data.

I could only leave Workday a few definitive suggestions based off of my learnings in this project:

  • One conclusion page design will probably not work with all of the edge cases- it may be a better idea to list multiple designs on the design system and have specific teams choose the design that most fit their users.
  • Push notification into inbox instead of archive. Perhaps make this use case specific.
  • The hybrid design seems to perform well with easy & medium tasks but there are still many use cases that haven’t been tested so the validity of the results could be skewed

Recommendations for Dublin's quick fix Slim Conclusion

  • The design should stay on the screen until an action is taken to account for accessibility users. Many users also prefered it this way.
  • Notification should be clickable to view more details on what was just submitted
  • Move to the middle so users still have access to action buttons

Recommendations for the current full conclusion page design:

  • Details and Process should be expanded already, remove empty fields
  • Needs green visual indicators to improve confidence in task completion
  • New users are confused by the meaning of “Up Next.” Some suggested “Submitted to...”
  • “Done” button reduces completion confidence. Users suggested “Close”

Overall, my internship at Workday gave me a priceless experience. I learned that not everything is straightforward as school projects and that it’s essential to make designs scalable. It’s totally possible that there may not be a single design that solves the problem at hand. I also learned a lot about how difficult it is to make changes to an enormous design system because it could cause unexpected effects to different use cases. I’m thankful for all of my teammates walking me through the complexities of designing in a larger organization.
